Here is a breakdown of why 2026 is a strong year to make the switch, along with the factors that might make you wait.

  

**1. The Financial "Sweet Spot" (Resale Value)**

- **Depreciation Curve:** Most cars lose the bulk of their value in the first 3 years. Between years 4 and 6, the depreciation curve flattens. In 2026, your 2021 car still has significant "trade-in" equity before it hits the next big value drop at the 7- or 8-year mark.
- **The 5-Year Maintenance Wall:** At 5 years, internal combustion engine (ICE) cars often require major, expensive services (e.g., timing belts, transmission flushes, or new sets of tires). Trading in now allows you to avoid these costs and put that money toward an EV down payment.

**2. Why 2026 is a Better Year for EVs than 2021 Was**

The EV landscape has matured significantly since you bought your current car:

- **Price Parity:** Many mid-range EVs now cost nearly the same as their petrol counterparts, especially when accounting for government incentives.
- **Battery Tech:** 2026 models feature faster charging and better cold-weather range than the early 2021 EVs.
- **Infrastructure:** Public charging networks are far more dense now than five years ago, reducing "range anxiety."

**3. Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) Comparison**

If you drive more than **12,000–15,000 km (approx. 8,000–10,000 miles)** per year, the switch usually pays for itself in fuel savings within 3–4 years.

|---|---|---|

**Reasons to Wait (The "Hold" Scenario)**

While 2026 is great, you might wait until **2027 or 2028** if:

1. **Solid-State Batteries:** You are holding out for the "next big leap." While 2026 has great tech, solid-state batteries (which offer even faster charging and longer life) are expected to enter the mass market around 2027–2028.
2. **Home Charging:** If your current residence doesn't allow for a home charger, the "hassle factor" of public charging might still outweigh the cost savings.
3. **Positive Equity:** If you still owe more on your 2021 car loan than it is worth ("underwater"), it is better to wait until the loan is paid off.
